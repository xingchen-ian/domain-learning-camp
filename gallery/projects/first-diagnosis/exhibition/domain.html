Domain & System Design

Why first aid? What are the rules?

The game is built on a specific learning shift: evaluate before treating. The domain, the data model, and the feedback rules below are the design backbone. The system graph in the brief is treated as the only primary reference; where a graph is normally included, this project explicitly skips it (per the student).

Domain Knowledge

First Aid as a real experience

The design brief comes from a high-school student's first-hand experience: first aid is rarely as clean as it looks in a textbook.

Novice misconception

Beginners often assume that all injuries can be treated the same way — that the goal is to act quickly, not to evaluate carefully. In reality, the wrong action in the wrong order makes the injury worse.

Expert judgment

An experienced first-aider pauses. They look at the injury type, depth, placement, and cleanliness. They ask what equipment they actually have. They reason about the order of operations, not just the speed.

The most important challenge in the domain

Equipment and incomplete information. Different injuries call for different tools: an x-ray for internal injuries, a specific medicine for a specific condition, a cast for a broken bone. In an emergency, not all of these may be available. The player must then decide with incomplete information — and live with the consequences.

Why this becomes playable

From real experience to game loop

The "evaluate before treating" shift maps cleanly into a game loop with three distinct phases: hidden information that must be revealed, a choice of tools that reflects what the player would actually have, and feedback that punishes wrong order rather than slow speed. The body becomes the scoreboard.

Hidden variables

Depth, cleanliness, and internal damage are not visible at the start. The player must choose to spend time examining before committing to a treatment.

Equipment that has to be found

The basic supplies (saline, small bandages, sutures, medicine) live in the sidebar and are always available. The situational supplies (casts, big bandages, cleaning cloths, water) are scattered around the disaster site and have to be picked up. Some treatments can't be done at all without first finding the right tool.

Wrong order = visible harm

Stitching before cleaning, basic stitching on a deep cut, or medicating a broken bone all produce immediate, readable consequences. The body answers back.

System Design

Data model

Three kinds of state are kept visibly separate in the code, as the brief instructs: environment data, player-controlled data, and system-calculated results.

Environment data (read by player)

  • Injury typecut / bruise / broken bone / internal
  • Locationhead, arm, leg, torso
  • Visible severitybleed rate, pain level, patient stability
  • World layoutjet, trees, fire patches, equipment spawns, patient positions

Player-controlled data

  • Medic positionx, y in the world; controlled by WASD/arrows
  • Equipment pick-upswhich env items have been collected
  • Treatment choiceswhich injury is selected, which tool is applied
  • Treatment timingwhen during the per-patient countdown the action is taken

System-calculated results

  • Patient stability0–100%, derived from untreated bleed & pain
  • Wound cleanlinesshidden until examined or cleaned
  • Wound depthhidden until examined
  • Stitch integritypops after a delay if not cleaned first
  • Per-patient clock170 s countdown, starts on find, keeps ticking during treatment
  • Mission statefound / lost / treated counters; lose at 3 patient deaths

Feedback translation

  • Bleeding continuesstitch was wrong order, or bandage not yet placed
  • Stitches popwound was not cleaned before stitching
  • Wound re-opensstitch too shallow for depth
  • Limb stays limpbroken bone not cast
  • Pain worsenswrong treatment for the injury type
  • Medicine is ignoredno water in inventory to take it with

Rules & outcomes

Win, lose, and the just-right range

Success

Stabilize every survivor

All seven patients are found, treated in the correct order, and discharged before their individual 170 s clock runs out. Mission ends with the success overlay.

Failure

Three ways to lose

A patient's per-patient clock runs out before treatment completes; a patient loses all stability from untreated damage; or three patients are lost in total — the mission ends regardless of the player's progress on the rest.

Just-right range

Too much is also wrong

Bandaging a wound that hasn't been stitched, suturing a wound that hasn't been cleaned, or using medicine where it doesn't apply all register as over-treatment and add to the patient's damage. The system punishes over-treatment, not just under-treatment.

Mission: Jet Crash — Forest Clearing

One mission, seven patients, one map

The first playable ships with a single mission but exercises the full system. Adding a second mission (e.g. a building collapse, a flood, a school cafeteria accident) is a matter of adding one entry to the LEVELS map and one mission card to game.html.

Map

Forest clearing with a crashed jet

A 1200×900 top-down world. Trees around the edges, the jet across the middle, fire patches on three sides, equipment inside and around the jet. The medic starts at the south end of the clearing.

  • World: 1200 × 900 px, 4:3 aspect ratio
  • Player speed: 220 px/s, AABB collision
  • Fire: 3 patches, animated
Patients

7 patients, 4 visible and 3 hidden

Four patients are visible from the start (Alex, Maya, Jordan, Drew). Three are hidden — Sam is behind the jet, Riley is in the trees, Casey is under debris — and only become visible when the medic walks within 100 px. The 170 s clock only starts when the medic walks within 60 px and finds them.

  • Reveal radius: 100 px
  • Find (clock-start) radius: 60 px
  • Timer: 170 s per patient, keeps ticking during treatment
Equipment

Two tiers, two different loops

Sidebar (always available): saline, small bandage, suture, medicine. These are the basic supplies a first-aider carries on them.
Environment (must find): cast, big bandage, cleaning cloth, water. These are scattered around the site — inside the jet, around the fire, in the undergrowth. Water is required to take medicine.

  • Sidebar items: unlimited use
  • Env items: one use each, picked up by walking over them
  • Medicine → water: explicit dependency

Feedback priorities

What the body shows the player

  • ImmediateBleeding rate, visible pain, limp limb, injury type, stability bar
  • DelayedStitch pop, wound re-opens, infection, swelling
  • State-basedStability bar, per-patient timer, equipment availability, found/lost counters, success / fail overlay
  • Audible (light)Find chime, stitch pop, success / fail chord, pickup chime, warn buzz
