Domain Knowledge: Have a Good Dream

The Real-World Experience

We all want to sleep well, but bad dreams can disturb our rest. After a nightmare, we wake up tired and start the day in a low mood. The old saying goes: "What you think about in the day, you will dream of at night." This suggests that what we do and experience during the day shapes our dreams at night — and therefore how we feel when we wake up.

But the connection isn't simple. Not everything that happens during the day appears in your dream. And not everything that seems positive actually leads to restful sleep. A day packed with exciting, stimulating activities might produce vivid dreams that leave you exhausted.

Novice Misconception

"If I just do lots of good, kind, fun things during the day, I'll have a great dream and wake up refreshed."

This is the intuitive approach — maximize "positive" activities. It works sometimes, but it misses two crucial details:

  • Personal fit: What counts as "good" depends on the person. An activity that helps one person might distress another.
  • Excitement threshold: Too much stimulation, even positive stimulation, can make dreams too intense and leave you tired.

Expert Judgment

Experts (people who understand sleep and dreaming well) know that:

  1. Attunement matters more than quantity. You need to notice what this person actually needs — their difficulties, their preferences, the details they reveal in what they say.
  2. Restraint is part of the skill. Choosing a few well-matched activities, rather than every available option, produces calmer, more restorative dreams.
  3. Reading feedback is essential. How the person responds — their words, their mood, their energy — tells you whether your choices truly helped.

Why This Becomes Playable

This domain is playable because the player must observe and interpret information (the character's words and reactions) before deciding what to do. It's not a guessing game — the character gives you clues, but they're subtle and personal. The challenge is moving from "do everything that looks nice" to "pay attention, choose carefully, and accept that less can be more."

The game makes this concrete: you see the character's mood and energy, you hear their reactions, you notice when an activity they seemed to enjoy didn't actually help their dream. Each run is short enough to experiment and adjust, so the learning loop — observe, judge, act, read feedback, adjust — happens naturally.

The Core Challenge

👥
Common vs. Personal
What most people think is "good" (parties, big social events) may not match what this character actually needs. The gap between common assumptions and personal reality is the heart of the game.
⚡
Excitement Threshold
A dream that is too exciting — even if it's "good" content — leaves you exhausted. The sweet spot between calm and stimulating is narrow and requires careful selection.
