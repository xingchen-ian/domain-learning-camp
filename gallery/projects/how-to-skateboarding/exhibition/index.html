How to Skateboarding

A web game that teaches the real skill behind skateboarding: reading the landform and adjusting your speed, core, and balance — not just standing on the board.

▶ Play the Game

Game Idea

Concept

When I got my skateboard as a present from my mother many years ago, I found that riding on the skateboard successfully isn't a beginner-friendly thing. It is all about adjusting my motion and changing my core from time to time, which depends on speed, angle, and landform.

Player Goal: Reach the destination without falling off the skateboard or running out of energy.

Core Learning Shift

Beginners think: skateboarding means standing on the board and moving as far as possible without falling down.

Experts know: skateboarding means reading the landforms and adjusting the speed, core, and balance in relation to it.

Domain Knowledge

Real-World Domain: Skateboarding

Keeping balance on a skateboard depends on the relationship between friction, landform, gravity, speed, and the position of your body's core (center of gravity).

Novice Misconception

Beginners often ignore the impact of gravity or friction on keeping balance. They tend to think they can keep balance as long as they are on the board.

Expert Judgment

Experts read the terrain ahead and shift their core proactively: raise the core when climbing uphill (lean forward for efficiency), and lower the core when going downhill (lean back for stability). They also choose an initial speed that matches the energy needed for the entire route.

Why This Becomes Playable

The invisible physics of skateboarding — gravity pulling you back on hills, friction slowing your wheels, the instability of a high center of mass — are translated into visible game variables the player can see, judge, and act on.

System Design

Environment Data

  • Landform — the shape of the ground (flat, hills)
  • Friction — how much the wheels resist motion
  • Wheel type — normal, ice (low friction), cake (high friction)
  • Energy pads — boost zones that restore speed

Player-Controlled Data

  • Initial speed — chosen before starting
  • Core height — raised or lowered during play (↑ / ↓)

System-Calculated Results

  • Speed — changes with friction, gravity, and core efficiency
  • Energy — derived from current speed
  • Balance — drops when core doesn't match the terrain
  • Position — how far along the route

Feedback Mapping

  • Speed → movement of the board + speed bar on HUD
  • Energy → energy bar (derived from speed)
  • Balance → character wobble + balance bar + core dot color (green / yellow / red)
  • Landform → visible terrain shape ahead + slope indicator

Success & Failure

  • Success: Reach the destination with balance above zero.
  • Failure — Fall: Balance drops to zero (wrong core position, especially on downhill).
  • Failure — Stuck: Speed drops to zero before reaching the destination (not enough initial energy, or inefficient core on uphill).

Challenge Presets

Level 1 — Flat Ground: Normal wheels, normal friction. Learn to keep the core centered and pick a reasonable speed.

Level 2 — Ice Hill: Ice wheels with very low friction. Speed is easy to keep, but balance is slippery and hard to recover. One hill to climb.

Level 3 — Cake Mountains: Cake wheels with strong friction. Speed is hard to keep across multiple hills, but balance is stable. An energy pad helps refill speed mid-route.
