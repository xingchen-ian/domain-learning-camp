To Leave

A game about packing, leaving, and what we carry with us.

▶ Play the Game

Game Idea

Concept

You are a packing agent helping people who are about to leave. Your job: pack their bag. But the real challenge isn't fitting everything — it's understanding who is leaving and what truly matters to them.

Player Goal

Pack the right combination of useful and emotional items to maximize the character's satisfaction. Balance Need (practical utility) and Want (emotional significance) by reading clues about the character.

Core Learning Shift

Beginners think packing means fitting everything. Experts learn to read the character first, then pack with intention — balancing what sustains life (Need) with what sustains the soul (Want).

Domain Knowledge

Real-World Domain

Packing bags is essential to everyday life — school bags, suitcases, moving boxes. The act of leaving is deeply connected to packing: leaving a place, memories, or time itself can be hard. This game explores that tension between usefulness and emotion.

Novice Misconception

"Everything can fit if I try hard enough." Also: packing is more important than understanding the person first.

Expert Judgment

Experts read clues about the person first — art, scribbles, fragments — and pack based on what the person truly needs and wants, not just what fits. They understand that letting go of old things is part of making room for new ones.

Why This Knowledge Becomes Playable

The packing constraint (limited space) creates genuine trade-offs. Understanding the character creates meaningful choices. The emotional weight of objects — revealed through memories — makes the decision feel real, not abstract.

The Three Cases

💼

The Wealthy Lady

Mrs. Wellington talks endlessly — about collections, trips, tastes. Her words are a flood, but hidden inside are clues about what truly matters. Voice style: Talkative (guides the player)

Container: Luxury Suitcase (8 slots) · 10 objects
🎓

The College Student

Sam doesn't talk much. Their clues are scribbles, doodles, and half-finished notes. You must interpret them yourself. Voice style: Quiet (minimal feedback)

Container: Dorm Backpack (6 slots) · 9 objects
👴

The Elderly Man

Mr. Chen says almost nothing. His clues are old photographs and faded newspaper clippings — fragments of a long, quiet life. Voice style: Silent (almost no guidance)

Container: Old Travel Bag (7 slots) · 10 objects
