Domain Knowledge

Real-World Domain: Packing Puzzle

Packing bags is essential to everyday life: school bags for daily use, suitcases for travel, moving boxes for relocation. The physical act of selecting and arranging items is something everyone does, but few think about why they choose what they choose.

The act of leaving is deeply connected to packing. Leaving a place, memories, or time itself involves deciding what to carry forward. Some things are necessary for survival; others carry emotional weight that makes life worth living. The tension between these two forces is the core of this game.

Novice Misconception

"Everything can fit if I try hard enough." — Beginners believe the challenge is purely spatial: arrange items cleverly, compress, stack, and everything goes in.

"Packing is more important than understanding the character." — Beginners focus on the container and the items, not on the person who is leaving.

Expert Judgment

Read the character first. Experts study the clues — art, scribbles, fragments — before deciding what to pack. Understanding who is leaving determines what matters.

Balance Need and Want. Experts know that pure utility (Need) and pure emotion (Want) both matter. A perfectly practical bag that ignores emotion leaves a person feeling empty; a purely emotional bag may leave them stranded.

Letting go is part of leaving. Experts accept that some things must be left behind — and that making room for new things requires releasing old ones.

Why This Knowledge Becomes Playable

The limited container creates genuine trade-offs. Understanding the character creates meaningful choices (not arbitrary ones). The emotional weight of objects — revealed through interactive memories — makes the decision feel real, not abstract. Each case presents a different character with different clues, so the player must adapt their strategy rather than apply one formula.

System Design

🌍 Environment Data

  • Object sizes — Each item occupies 1–3 container slots. Larger items create harder trade-offs.
  • Container capacity — Each case has a different bag/suitcase with limited slots (6–8).
  • Character clues — Text notes, sketches, doodles, photographs. The player must interpret them.
  • Object memories — Each item has a memory the player can enter, revealing emotional significance.

🎮 Player-Controlled Data

  • Select objects — Click an item to see its details, Need/Want scores, and clue connection.
  • Enter memories — Step into an object's memory to experience its emotional weight.
  • Pack / Unpack — Move items into or out of the container. When full, must remove to add more.
  • Read clues — Open the clue panel to review character information at any time.
  • Finish packing — Decide when to stop and see the results.

⚙️ System-Calculated Results

  • Need total — Sum of Need scores for all packed items. Measures practical preparedness.
  • Want total — Sum of Want scores for all packed items. Measures emotional care.
  • Satisfaction — Weighted combination of Need% and Want%, penalized for extreme imbalance. Each character has a threshold (70–75%).
  • Character reaction — Different dialogue based on satisfaction level (high/medium/low).

Feedback Mappings

Immediate
Container full → cannot add more items. Voice line from character on each pack/unpack action.
Discovered over time
Need/Want scores revealed in item detail panel. Clue connections shown when examining items. Memory scenes reveal emotional significance.
End-of-game
Total Need/Want breakdown. Satisfaction percentage. Character reaction quote. Specific feedback about what was missing (low Need or low Want).

Success & Failure Conditions

Success: Satisfaction above the character's threshold (70–75% depending on case). The character gives a warm, understanding reaction.

Failure (low satisfaction): The character is disappointed. The player receives specific feedback about what was missing — too few practical items, too few emotional items, or both.

No hard fail: The player doesn't "lose" — they always get a result. But low satisfaction clearly signals they didn't understand the character, encouraging a retry with better observation.

Challenge Presets — What Changes Between Cases

Case 1: Wealthy Lady

  • Container: 8 slots (large suitcase)
  • 10 objects, sizes 1–3
  • Talkative character — gives feedback on every action
  • Clues are explicit text and art
  • Key challenge: She talks so much that the real clues are buried in noise

Case 2: College Student

  • Container: 6 slots (smaller backpack)
  • 9 objects, sizes 1–2
  • Quiet character — minimal verbal feedback
  • Clues are scribbles and doodles (harder to interpret)
  • Key challenge: The "REAL" letter vs. the "copy" — same content, different emotional weight

Case 3: Elderly Man

  • Container: 7 slots (medium travel bag)
  • 10 objects, sizes 1–3
  • Silent character — almost no verbal feedback
  • Clues are old photographs and newspaper clippings
  • Key challenge: Some items (chair, clock) can't fit — accepting what must be left behind
