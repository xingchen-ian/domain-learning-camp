Rock Climbing

A web game that teaches the real skill behind climbing: reading the rock, managing your stamina, and finding rest — not just pulling yourself up as fast as you can.

▶ Play the Game

Game Idea

Concept

You are a rock climber on a vertical wall. Your goal is to climb as high as you can without running out of stamina. The wall has different types of holds — some are easy to grip, others are small or wobbly. You must read each hold, choose your path, and decide when to push forward and when to rest.

Player Goal

Reach the top of the wall — or climb higher than your previous best. There is no single "win." Instead, you improve through better technique: smarter hold selection, better grip control, and wiser energy management.

Core Learning Shift

Novice: Climbing means grabbing the next hold and pulling yourself up as fast as possible.

Expert: Climbing means reading the rock's shape and texture, positioning the body efficiently, finding rests, and managing energy.

Domain Knowledge

The Real Experience

Rock climbing is difficult because reaching a destination depends on the environment — rock structure, rock texture, and wind intensity. A beginner who joined a climbing lesson quickly realizes that climbing is not about speed. It is about making the right decisions under physical and mental pressure.

Novice Misconception

Beginners think they should climb as fast as possible. They reach for the next obvious hold and pull up immediately, even when their arms are tired. They ignore rest positions, use their arms too much instead of their legs, and keep climbing straight up without checking the quality or shape of holds first.

Expert Judgment

Experts read the rock's shape and texture before committing. They position their body efficiently to save energy. They identify and use resting positions. They plan short sequences rather than climbing move-by-move. They make calmer decisions under fatigue and know when to push through versus when to recover.

Why This Becomes Playable

Hold Types as Decisions

Each hold type (jug, crimp, sloper, pinch, rest) has a different stamina cost and stability impact. The player must learn to read holds visually and choose paths that balance difficulty with energy conservation.

Grip Force as Skill

A grip meter challenges the player to find the "just-right" tension: gripping too lightly risks slipping, gripping too hard wastes energy. This directly mirrors the real-world skill of optimal grip force.

Stamina as the Core Constraint

Every move costs stamina. Rest holds recover it. The player learns that skipping rests early leads to getting pumped and falling later — even if each individual move felt fine.

System Design

Core Player Loop: Observe → Judge → Act → Feedback → Adjust

Observe

Rock shape, texture, and orientation. Wall features. Possible foot placements. Current body position. How pumped the arms feel. Potential resting spots. Distance and difficulty to the next hold or rest.

Judge

Is a hold reliable enough to use? Is the current body position efficient? Can I reach the next hold without overextending? Should I rest now? Is my planned sequence still good given my current fatigue?

Act

Move hands and feet to different holds. Adjust body position. Rest to recover energy. Choose when to push through a section or slow down. Set grip force on each move.

Read Feedback

Body position (shaky or stable). Grip security (hold wobbles or hand slips). Successful vs. failed moves with clear animation. Smooth vs. unstable movement flow.

Adjust

If arms shake → find a rest. If body feels unstable → adjust foot placement. If a move fails → try a different hold, body posture, or path. Learn from each attempt.

Data Model

Environment Data

Hold type (jug, crimp, sloper, pinch, rest), hold quality (solid, wobbly), hold position, wall height, route configuration, wind conditions.

Player-Controlled Data

Which hold to move to, grip force level, when to rest, when to adjust position, pace of climbing, route selection.

System-Calculated Results

Stamina level, stability level, current height, body shake intensity, lean amount, grip success/failure, movement smoothness, fall/summit state.

Feedback Translation
  • Fatigue signal: Visible forearm shaking, slower movement, declining green energy bar. Tells the player they are not resting enough.
  • Stability signal: Character leans backward, feet slip. Tells the player their position is inefficient and wasting energy.
  • Efficiency signal: Smooth flowing animation when good, versus stopping and struggling when inefficient. Tells the player whether their sequence is working.
Success & Failure

Success

Reach the top hold, or climb higher than your previous best. Success improves through better technique — not just raw speed.

Failure

Stamina reaches zero while NOT on a rest hold. If you are on a rest when exhausted, you can still recover. But if you get fully pumped in an exposed position with no rest nearby, you fall. Your highest point is still recorded.

Challenge Presets (3 Routes)

🟢 First Ascent

Beginner-friendly. Mostly jug holds, several rest spots, few wobbly holds. Larger optimal grip zone. Standard stamina drain. Teaches basic stamina management and grip control.

🟡 Crux Master

Technical route. Mix of crimps, slopers, and pinches. 32% of holds are wobbly. Fewer rests. Standard stamina drain. Smaller optimal grip zone. Teaches reading holds and choosing efficient paths.

🔴 The Marathon

Endurance test. 22 holds, long route, few rests. 15% faster stamina drain. Forces the player to manage pace carefully — moving too fast burns out before the next rest.

Just-Right Ranges
  • Grip force: Too light → slip risk. Too hard → 1.7× stamina cost. Optimal → 0.75× cost. There is a sweet spot.
  • Pace: Moving too fast burns stamina before the next rest. Moving too slowly wastes energy holding position. A good rhythm exists.
  • Rest timing: Resting too early wastes time. Resting too late means you fall. Each rest has diminishing returns.
