Domain Knowledge: Basic Color Theory

The real-world knowledge that powers Hue Keeper, and how it becomes playable.

The Real-World Domain

Color theory is the study of how colors interact, mix, and contrast. In art class, we learned that the three primary colors — Red, Blue, and Yellow — cannot be created by mixing other colors. When you mix two primaries, you get a secondary color:

  • Red + Blue = Purple
  • Red + Yellow = Orange
  • Blue + Yellow = Green

Beyond mixing, complementary colors are pairs that sit opposite each other on the color wheel: Red–Green, Blue–Orange, Yellow–Purple. These pairs create the strongest contrast and visual tension when placed side by side.

In real life, basic color theory helps people pick furniture that matches their home renovation, choose outfits that look coordinated, and create artwork with intentional visual impact.

Novice Misconception

Beginners often presume that everything in the game (including the monsters) should be solved by only matching with the same colors. They think "red problem = red solution."

This misconception is directly challenged in Level 3: using the same color as a monster makes it stronger. The player must unlearn "same = good" and discover that complementary (opposite) colors are the solution — just as a conflicting color creates visual tension in art.

Expert Judgment

An expert in color theory doesn't just match — they judge, mix, and match simultaneously. They look at a situation and quickly decide:

  • Do I have the exact color I need? If yes, use it directly (matching).
  • Can I mix two nearby colors to get what I need? If the exact color is far away, mixing might save time (mixing under pressure).
  • What color defeats this monster? Not the same color — the complementary opposite (conflicting approach).

This judgment happens under time and space constraints. The expert weighs efficiency (is it faster to run far for the exact color, or mix two nearby ones?) against safety (in Level 3, can I afford the time to mix when a monster is approaching?).

Why This Knowledge Becomes Playable

⏱ Time Pressure

The fresco fades over time (Levels 2–3) and monsters approach (Level 3). This forces quick decisions — exactly the kind of rapid judgment an expert makes.

📎 Space Constraints

Flowers are scattered across the garden. The fountain sits in the middle. The player must judge: run far for the exact color, or mix two nearby primaries? This trains spatial efficiency.

⚠ Risk vs. Reward

In Level 3, the same color strengthens monsters while the complementary color defeats them. The player learns through immediate, visceral feedback that "same" is not always "correct."

🔄 The Mixing Loop

The fountain mechanic makes color mixing a physical action: collect two primaries, walk to the fountain, receive the secondary. This bodily encoding helps the mixing rules stick.

Core Learning Shift

Before: "I need to find the same color as the problem."
After: "I need to judge the situation, mix if needed, and use complementary colors strategically — all under time pressure."

This shift from simple matching to comprehensive color judgment is the heart of Hue Keeper. The game mechanics (fading, mixing, monster weaknesses) are designed to make this shift feel natural and earned through play.
