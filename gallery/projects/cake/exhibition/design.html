Domain & System Design

Why baking, what real-world knowledge the game teaches, and how the system graph becomes a playable loop.

The Real-World Domain

Baking a cake is a real sequence of decisions, not just decoration. You read the order, choose a base and a frosting, decide how many layers to bake, then add toppings. Skipping any step shows up in the final cake.

I used to bake with my mother on birthdays. I enjoyed decorating the cakes and learned that baking requires following steps carefully. That hands-on feeling is what this game tries to capture.

Novice Misconception

Beginners think that finishing as fast as possible, or making the cake look good, is the most important thing. In real baking, the order comes first. The fastest cake still has to match what the customer asked for.

Expert Judgment

Experienced bakers do three things in order:

  1. Read the order — the order tells you the base, frosting, layers, and toppings.
  2. Plan — decide the smallest correct set of actions and the order to do them in.
  3. Build — mix, bake, decorate, then check the cake against the order before serving.

The game models each of these as a step you can act on, not just a tip.

Why This Knowledge Becomes Playable

The game turns the invisible habit of "read the order first" into a real input (A). The timer turns planning into a measurable cost. The customer face turns the final check into feedback you can see.

System Graph

The graph below shows the full observe → judge → act → feedback loop.

Win or Game Over. Win: all customer requirements met, time > 0. Game Over: missing requirements or time = 0.

Player input. Read customer order (A key), select ingredients (mouse), put cake into oven (W key), add decorations (mouse), submit cake (Space).

Game environment. Timer, customer requirements. Difficulty preset changes the time limit and how many requirements the customer has.

Exposed data on screen. Timer, cake progress, ingredients available, customer patience level.

Three difficulty presets:

  • Level 1. Finish the cake based on requirements. No timer.
  • Level 2. Finish the cake based on the 1-minute time limit.
  • Level 3. Finish the cake based on requirements and a 1-minute time limit. You must manually pick a different base for each of the 3 layers.

Data Model

Environment Data

  • Customer order & requirements
  • Available ingredients & decorations
  • Current cake stage (mixing, baking, decorating)
  • Remaining time

Player-Controlled Data

  • Selected base
  • Selected frosting
  • Layers set at mix time
  • Decorations added / removed

System-Calculated Results

  • Cake visual appearance
  • Customer satisfaction
  • Time remaining
  • Win or failure at the end of the level

Feedback Translation

  • Cake appearance. The cake on the canvas updates whenever you add an ingredient or decoration. You can also tell at a glance whether the cake differs from the order.
  • Countdown timer. The timer sits at the top of the screen. It turns yellow with 20 seconds left and red with 5 seconds left.
  • Customer reaction. When you submit, the customer face smiles for a correct cake and frowns for a wrong one. The order card shows checkmarks or X marks for each requirement.

Rules, Boundaries, and Outcomes

Important states. Unmixed / mixed. Baked / unbaked. Decorated / not decorated. Patient / impatient. Satisfied / unsatisfied. Incomplete / complete. Success / failure.

How player actions change the system.

  • Mixing the batter takes 10 seconds. A progress bar fills up. You cannot do anything else while mixing.
  • Baking in the oven takes 10 seconds. The oven light turns on and a progress bar fills. You cannot do anything else while baking.
  • Adding toppings changes the state from not decorated to decorated.
  • Submitting changes customer satisfaction.

Feedback during the game.

  • While mixing, a progress bar fills under the Mix button and the stage message says "The batter is mixing… wait for it!"
  • While baking, a progress bar fills under the Oven button and the stage message says "The cake is baking… patience!"
  • When the level timer runs out, the game immediately shows "Game Over" and freezes.
  • Time ticks down each second when a timer is active.

    • Success condition. Meet all customer requirements before the time limit.

    Failure conditions. Submit a cake that does not match the order, or run out of time before completing the cake.

    Challenge Presets

    Each level changes the system, not just the look. The time limit and the number of requirements are real variables.

    • Level 1. No timer. 1 layer. 1 topping. Teaches reading the order.
    • Level 2. 1 minute timer. 2 layers. 2 toppings. Teaches planning.
    • Level 3. 1-minute timer. 3 layers (player picks a different base for each layer manually). 3 toppings. Teaches multi-layered planning and memorization under time pressure.
