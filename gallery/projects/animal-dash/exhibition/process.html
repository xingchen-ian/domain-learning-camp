Key Design Decisions

Why Track A (No Build Step)?

Pure HTML/CSS/Canvas 2D with no npm, Vite, or external libraries. Exhibition works on an iMac without Node.js, npm install, or internet access.

Why 14 Animals with 7 Environments?

Each animal has a unique adaptation profile across 7 terrain types. Dramatic feedback — a Cheetah is perfect on grassland but useless in the ocean. Adaptation is HIDDEN to force experiential learning.

Why Hidden Adaptation?

Showing adaptation percentages would let beginners just pick the highest number — that's exactly the novice misconception the game is designed to correct. By hiding it, players must observe speed changes, stamina drain rates, and HP loss patterns to learn which animals fit which terrains.

Why Strong Opponent?

The opponent has 1.5× stamina, 1.3× HP, slower drain, and stamina regeneration. This ensures the opponent won't die easily — the primary victory condition is outrunning them by distance, not waiting for them to collapse. This makes the game more competitive and pushes players to focus on their own animal choices and survival strategy.

Why Rich Environment Visuals?

Each terrain now has layered sky/ground gradients, ambient particles (snow, sand, bubbles, leaves), and decorative side objects. This makes terrain transitions more obvious — especially important for Level 2 & 3 where terrain changes aren't announced. Players should be able to recognize environments visually, not just by reading text.

Why "Animal Dash"?

The student chose the name "Animal Dash" for the project. It's punchier and more game-like than "Racing Animals."
