An Adventure on Horseback

Riding with judgment. Riding with trust.

You are about to ride a real horse - not control a cartoon on a flat track. Three courses, three horses, three lessons. Speed, line, take-off point, and the feel of a living animal under you. Welcome to An Adventure on Horseback.

Play the Game

Game Idea

Player Goal

Ride your horse through a course of show-jumping obstacles and reach the finish flag. Time, route, take-off point and the horse's mood all matter. Clear the course with your horse willing to work with you.

Core Learning Shift

The naive idea is: the faster the better, the shorter the line the better. After 4 years of real horse-riding, the player learns that riding needs a decisive, feasible and reasonable judgment, and a horse is an organism with its own feelings. You have to read your horse, not just push it.

Domain Knowledge

Real-World Domain

After 4 years of riding, the student has learned three layers of skill: beginners must learn the feel of the horse, intermediate riders must pick the right speed, line and take-off point, and advanced riders must build a deep relationship with a horse that has its own mood.

Novice Misconception

Beginners think: speed up, take the shortest line, force the horse over the fence. This almost always knocks down the bar, runs out of time on the corners, or turns the horse against you.

Expert Judgment

A good rider plans the line before the obstacle, picks a stable working speed, and chooses a take-off point that lets the horse see the fence and still has time to fold its front legs. The horse's mood sets the ceiling.

Why This Becomes Playable

Each mechanic in the game is a real riding decision: speed has a sweet spot, the route has a workable line, the take-off point has a window, and the horse's emotion is a live system that you must manage, not override.

Three Levels, Three Lessons

Level 1 - First Canter

Beginner No timer Gentle horse 8 fences

A straight route across an open meadow with 8 show-jumping fences. No time pressure — learn the feel of speed, line and take-off point on a willing horse at your own pace.

Level 2 - Winding Path

Intermediate 90s limit 10 mixed fences

An S-shaped course across wide grassland with verticals, oxers and walls. Plan your line through 10 obstacles before the clock runs out.

Level 3 - Trust the Bond

Advanced 80s limit Spirited horse 14 fences

A long roundabout course on a strong but bad-tempered mare with 14 fences. Whip her and she will refuse. Soothe her and she will clear the whole course.

The Core Challenge: Your Horse May Not Listen

Disobedience System

This is the heart of the game. A real horse is a living creature, not a machine. When you scare it or tire it out, it stops listening to your hands.

Horse Emotion Obedience What Happens
Happy (≥75) 100% Horse responds perfectly to all inputs
Calm (50–74) 52%–93% Slight lag in steering; still willing to jump
Tired (25–49) 11%–50% Steering barely works; random drift; may refuse jumps
Angry (<25) ≤11% Horse ignores reins, drifts wildly, refuses most jumps, may bolt

The game shows a red warning banner when your horse stops listening. Press E (Soothe) to raise her mood. Avoid F (Whip) unless absolutely necessary — every whip makes things worse.

Before You Ride: Intro & Camera

Story intro

The game opens on a short story screen that sets the scene: a horse is a feeling creature, not a machine. It previews the three lessons — read the horse, plan your line, keep calm. Press Space or click to continue to the menu.

Camera views (press V)

While playing, press V to cycle three perspectives:

  • Behind (default) — a low follow-cam over the horse's tail. The horse faces each fence head-on, so you meet the jump square instead of sideways — the realistic way a rider approaches a fence.
  • Top-down — a high, near-bird's-eye angle across the open meadow, good for reading your line and spacing.
  • Side — the classic 3/4 view that shows the horse's full stride and jump arc.

The fences are sized to real show-jumping scale (the top rail sits at roughly the horse's withers height, about 0.85–1.10× the horse's own scale) and spaced generously apart — roughly 85–115 world units between fences — so you have room to rebalance and set up a clean approach.
