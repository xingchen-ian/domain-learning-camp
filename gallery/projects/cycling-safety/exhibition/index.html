Cycling Safety

A bike riding game where rushing leads to crashes. Learn to control your speed, read the road, and steer around danger — just like in real cycling.

Play the Game

Game Idea

Concept

You are cycling on a trail toward a destination. The road changes terrain, curves, and throws obstacles in your path. Your job is to observe what's ahead, judge whether your speed is safe, and adjust before it's too late.

Player Goal

Reach the finish line within the time limit — without crashing into obstacles, losing balance, or running out of time.

Core Learning Shift At first, players think the main goal is to rush to the destination, but that will easily cause them to fall. When they become experts, they know how to avoid danger by controlling speed and direction.
Why This Domain? "I often go cycling with my parents. I once tripped over a wooden stick on the ground, and I realized that awareness of your surroundings directly affects your speed and bike-handling skills." — Cici chen

Domain Knowledge: Cycling

Real-World Experience Cycling is not just about pedaling forward. A skilled rider constantly observes the road, adjusts speed before curves, avoids obstacles, and adapts to different surfaces (pavement, gravel, wet roads).
Novice Misconception Beginners think riding a bike is mostly about pedaling forward. They rush toward obstacles, forget to adjust direction, or fail to control speed.
Expert Judgment Experts understand the relationship between obstacle, speed, direction, and route planning. They slow down before curves, lean into turns, and pick safe lines through obstacles.
Why It Becomes Playable The core challenge — matching speed to road conditions — maps directly to game mechanics. Speed, stability, terrain, and obstacles create a system where careful observation is rewarded and rushing is punished.

System Design

The game is built around three data categories, clearly separated in the code:

Category Data Description
Environment Data Terrain type, curve shape, obstacles, slope The road ahead — what the player observes and reacts to.
Player-Controlled Data Speed (pedal/brake), direction (steer), lean angle The actions the player takes in response to the environment.
System-Calculated Results Stability, shake, skid marks, collisions, win/lose Feedback the player reads to judge whether their actions were correct.

Feedback Mappings

Bike Shaking / Handlebar Vibration Comes from taking a curve too fast or entering rough terrain. Appears as screen vibration and visual bike wobble. Immediate feedback.
Loss of Traction / Skidding Comes from tire-ground contact on slippery surfaces or hard braking. Appears as skid marks on the ground and a screeching sound. Discovered over time — not instant crash, but leads to loss of control.
Speed Feedback Shown through scroll speed, wind sound (volume rises with speed), and speed lines at high velocity.
Crash / "You Failed" Hitting a rock or large obstacle causes an instant crash. Stability dropping to zero means the rider falls. Both show a clear failure screen with a tip.

Win / Lose Conditions

Win Reach the destination without crashing, maintain balance (stability stays above 0), and arrive within the time limit.
Game Over Crash into an obstacle (rock = instant crash), lose balance and fall (stability reaches 0), run out of time, or drop below minimum speed for too long.

Challenge Presets (Levels)

Each level changes system variables and relationships — not just visual decoration:

Level Terrain Obstacles Curves Key Challenge
1: Park Path Flat pavement Few small sticks Gentle, wide turns Learn basic steering and obstacle avoidance
2: Mixed Trail Pavement + gravel Rocks, branches, sticks Moderate turns Gravel reduces grip — slow down on loose surface
3: Hilly Gravel Hilly gravel + rough Scattered rocks Sharp turns Control speed on descents; slopes affect momentum
4: Mountain Pass Rough + slippery Dense obstacles Tight, continuous turns Combine all skills; slippery surface causes skidding

Core Player Learning Loop

1. Observe
See the obstacle and curve shape ahead
2. Judge
Is my speed safe? Is my direction correct?
3. Act
Turn, accelerate, or brake
4. Read Feedback
Bike shakes? Slow down. Crashed? Change direction next time.
5. Adjust
Decrease speed on curves. Steer around obstacles. Adapt to terrain.
