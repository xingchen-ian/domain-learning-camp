Section One

Domain Knowledge

Chemistry and escape rooms seem unrelated, but both reward the same habit: read the situation first, then solve it with minimal tools. This page explains the real-world domain, common beginner traps, and the expert judgment the game aims to cultivate.

🔬 Real Domain: Chemistry

Chemistry is the science of what happens when substances meet. A reaction is not just "two things in a beaker" — it depends on order, temperature, and whether the mixture is filtered or evaporated. Pouring the wrong thing first creates side reactions. Skipping filtration leaves solid waste in the product. Forgetting evaporation lets the solvent dilute the result.

In a school lab, the answer is on the procedure sheet. In this game, the procedure sheet is hidden in the room — you must find it, read it, and follow it under time pressure while spirits roam nearby.

🚪 Real Domain: Escape Rooms

An escape room is a compact puzzle space. The exit is rarely the hardest part — the hardest part is gathering the right information fragments in time. Clues are scattered across the room: graffiti on the desk, mirror-written messages, chemical labels on bottles. The room is designed so that beginners panic, confuse clues, and waste the first ten minutes. Experts slow down, inventory what they have, and only act when they know what they're looking for.

Common Pitfalls

What Beginners Get Wrong

The most common trap is a mental shortcut: the goal is to escape, so head for the exit. In a chemistry escape room, that instinct is exactly backwards. The exit won't open until you complete the formula, and the formula is hidden in clues that beginners skip.

🏃 Running for the Exit

Beginner: "I see the portal. I'm going there."

Reality: The portal is locked. It won't open until the reagent mixing is complete.

🧪 Dumping Everything In

Beginner: "I'll just pour everything in and heat it."

Reality: Wrong order = purple smoke, health loss, wasted reagents.

👀 Ignoring the Room

Beginner: "I just need to collect the colorful bottles."

Reality: Three of them are decoys. The formula only requires two.

Section Two

System Design

The game is built as an explicit system with environment data, player-controlled data, and computed results — with clear feedback transformations between them. The mapping below reflects the original design whiteboard.

Environment Data Player-Controlled Data Computed Results
  • Lighting (visibility & atmosphere)
  • Spirits — appear/disappear, speed, count
  • Items — chemical reagents, tools (clues & instruments)
  • Zone status: Safe / Dangerous, easy / hard to escape
  • Direct space / spirit spawn space
  • Physical actions: move forward/back, jump/climb, crouch/crawl
  • Chemical actions (lab bench): select correct chemicals & equipment, mix reagents, filter mixture, heat, evaporate solvent
  • Health lost — number of character injuries
  • Total completion time
  • Types/amounts of chemicals used
  • Win / lose state

✅ Success Conditions

Find the exit portal and escape within the time limit using the correct sequence of chemicals and tools — and finish before health reaches zero.

❌ Failure Conditions

Time runs out, caught by spirits too many times, or health hits zero. A failed attempt is not the end — it's data for the next plan.

Feedback Transformations

How the Dream Talks to You

Invisible states are converted into visible, readable feedback. Players always know why they lost and what to try next.

💔 Health Bar

Any damage — purple smoke, spirit contact, wrong tool order — deducts from the health bar. Health depleted = dream ends.

⏱ Countdown Timer

Time is the second resource. Below 20 seconds the timer turns orange; below 10 it flashes. Time runs out and the round ends.

🧪 Chemical Counter

Shows in real time how many reagents you've picked up vs. what the formula requires. The formula is the goal — extra reagents waste time.

💨 Purple Smoke

Wrong lab bench operations produce purple/pink smoke rising from the beaker. Health deducted.

✨ Sparkle Particles

Successful chemical operations release sparkle, heart, and star particles drifting across the screen. A visual cue telling you you did it right!

📳 Damage Flash

Any damage to the player triggers a full-screen coral flash and screen shake — instant "I did something wrong" feedback.

Challenge Presets

How Difficulty Is Encoded

The three levels aren't just reskins. They change real system variables, so players experience different puzzles, not just different wallpaper.

Level 01
First-Aid Neutralization
Changed variables: time 60s, 2 spirits, 2 reagents, 1-step tool. Players learn the game loop under zero pressure, at a friendly pace.
Level 02
Wastewater Treatment
Changed variables: time 50s, 3 spirits, 2 reagents, 3-step tools (mix → filter → heat). Adds multi-step reasoning.
Level 03
Darkroom Silver Recovery
Changed variables: time 60s, 4 fast spirits, 3 reagents, 4-step tools. Only those who read the room carefully will escape.
