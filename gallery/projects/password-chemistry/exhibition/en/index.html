Core Concept

Chemistry × Escape Room

I am a middle school student who loves chemistry. After playing an escape room with my family, I realized that the game was never just about reaching the exit — it requires reading the environment, spotting subtle clues, and using the right tool at the right moment. So I thought: what if the tools were chemicals?

1

Observe

Glowing bottles, wandering spirits, items on the ground. Clues hide in plain sight.

2

Judge

Is this bottle useful? Is this spirit friendly? Is this path safe or dangerous?

3

Act

Move, pick up, mix, filter, heat, evaporate. Execute the right sequence.

4

Read Feedback

Health bar, timer, chemical counter, particle effects. The dream always responds.

5

Adjust

Running out of time? Try a faster route. Failed? Think outside the box.

→

Learn

On the second attempt, you read faster, choose more accurately, and use fewer reagents.

Learning Shift

From "Rushing to the Exit" to "Reading the Room"

Beginners think escaping is the goal. Experts know that escaping is simply the natural result of reading the environment, making a plan, and using every tool — including chemicals — at the right moment.

🌸 Beginner Pitfalls

Beelining for the exit, picking up anything that glows, ignoring recipe steps, hoping the portal opens on its own. When spirits close in, beginners panic-pour everything into the beaker, wasting limited reagents.

🧪 Expert Judgment

Reading recipes in the lab. Identifying which glowing bottles are actually needed. Planning a route through safe zones. Mixing only in the order the recipe demands, using just enough heat.

Three Challenge Presets

From First Aid Room to Darkroom

The game ships with three playable levels. Each level changes real variables — time, NPC count and speed, recipe complexity — not just the decor.

Why It Works

Real Skills, Wrapped in a Dream

Real Chemistry, Real Sequence

Operations must follow a realistic order: add the right reagent, mix, then filter/heat/evaporate. Wrong sequence means wasted reagents and lost health — just like a real lab mistake.

Failure Teaches You

Purple smoke, lost health, spirit encounters, a shrinking health bar — these are the dream's honest responses to poor planning. Failure is data, not punishment.

Second Time's Better

You learn to read the room faster, plan your route, and pick up only the minimum reagents needed. The skill transfers: read first, act later.
